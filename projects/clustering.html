A Study of Clustering Algorithms with Experimental Evaluation on Several Datasets

Team project — completed collaboratively

A systematic experimental evaluation of eight representative clustering methods — K-Means, Agglomerative Clustering (Ward, Complete, Average), Gaussian Mixture Model (Full, Tied), Spectral Clustering (RBF), and DBSCAN — across four standard datasets: Iris, Wine, Breast Cancer Wisconsin, and Digits.

K-Means Agglomerative Clustering Gaussian Mixture Model Spectral Clustering DBSCAN PCA / t-SNE / UMAP

Introduction

In the age of digital transformation, data has become one of the most important resources for organizations, businesses, and scientific research. The rapid growth of information systems, sensors, and digital platforms has produced enormous volumes of data in many different forms. However, most data collected in practice comes without clear labels or classification information, making knowledge extraction a major challenge. In this context, unsupervised learning methods play a key role in uncovering the hidden structure of data and supporting data-driven analysis and decision-making.

Clustering is one of the most fundamental and widely used problems in unsupervised learning. Its goal is to group data objects with similar characteristics into the same cluster while ensuring clear separation between different clusters. Unlike supervised classification, clustering does not require the input data to be pre-labeled.

Motivated by these needs, this report systematically studies the theoretical foundations and experimental workflow of clustering on tabular data. The content is organized as a complete data-analysis pipeline: preprocessing, dimensionality reduction, model building, result visualization, and performance evaluation. Through experiments on well-known benchmark datasets — Iris, Wine, Digits, and Breast Cancer — the report aims to clarify the mechanics, characteristics, and applicability of each clustering method across different data contexts.

Experimental Design

The study strictly follows the unsupervised principle: labels are kept completely sealed throughout preprocessing, dimensionality reduction, training, and hyperparameter selection, and are only revealed once, at the final extrinsic evaluation stage — mirroring real deployment conditions where labels are usually unavailable or prohibitively expensive to collect. The number of clusters K is chosen using the Silhouette-Optimum strategy (K* = argmaxK∈{2,...,14} Sil(K)), then compared against the true number of groups Ktrue.

Conclusion

This study presents a systematic experimental evaluation of eight representative clustering methods — K-Means, Agglomerative Clustering (Ward, Complete, Average), Gaussian Mixture Model (Full, Tied), Spectral Clustering (RBF), and DBSCAN — across four benchmark datasets widely used in the machine learning community: Iris, Wine, Breast Cancer Wisconsin, and Digits. The entire workflow follows a unified seven-step pipeline, strictly observing the unsupervised principle. This approach ensures objectivity and a fair comparison across methods, while reflecting real deployment conditions where labels are usually unavailable or prohibitively expensive to collect.

Accuracy of Silhouette-Optimum in Selecting K

The results show this method achieves 75% accuracy: K*Sil = Ktrue on Wine (K=3), Breast Cancer (K=2), and Digits (K=10), with a single mismatch on Iris (K*Sil=2 vs. Ktrue=3). The most notable case is Digits: in a 64-dimensional space with 10 target classes, Silhouette-Optimum still correctly recovers K*=10 — a non-trivial result, since the curse of dimensionality typically flattens distance structure in high-dimensional spaces. The Iris case is not a methodological failure: Iris is widely documented in the literature for the significant overlap between the Iris versicolor and Iris virginica species, so Silhouette-Optimum is faithfully reflecting the data's geometric structure rather than making an error.

The Breakdown Between Intrinsic and Extrinsic Evaluation

The central and most methodologically significant finding of this study is that the correlation between the Silhouette Score and the Adjusted Rand Index is not constant — it varies sharply with each dataset's geometric properties, including cases of strong negative correlation, where the intrinsic metric ranks algorithms in the opposite direction from their true quality.

The most severe case is Breast Cancer (ρ=−0.928): Agglomerative Complete and Spectral RBF achieve the highest Silhouette scores (0.661) but ARI ≈ 0 — these two methods find compact, tight clusters that do not correspond at all to the clinical boundary between malignant and benign cells. This result carries a serious methodological implication: in problems with a clear applied semantic — healthcare, molecular biology, finance, customer segmentation — using the Silhouette Score as the sole model-selection metric can lead to choosing the wrong algorithm, with serious real-world consequences.

Performance Comparison Across Algorithm Families

  • K-Means: stable, consistent performance (ARI 0.534–0.898), the fastest compute time; its spherical-cluster assumption is not a serious limitation when the data's structure fits.
  • Agglomerative Ward: stands out most in high-dimensional space — on Digits (64D) it reaches ARI=0.664, clearly outperforming K-Means, GMM Full, and Spectral RBF, despite having the lowest Silhouette score.
  • Agglomerative Complete/Average: fail systematically on high-dimensional data (ARI≈0 on Digits) due to the chaining effect.
  • GMM Tied achieves the highest ARI on Wine (0.946, only 3/178 samples misclassified) and the highest overall mean ARI — the tied covariance constraint acts as effective regularization.
  • DBSCAN: shows sharply binary behavior depending on dimensionality — strong on Iris/Wine (low dimensionality, with outliers) but on Wine it must discard 65.7% of samples as noise to reach a high ARI; on Digits it discards up to 96.3% of samples, making the ARI=1.0 result nothing more than a statistical artifact.
  • Spectral RBF: never achieves competitive performance on any dataset (mean ARI 0.255) — the RBF kernel with a fixed bandwidth isn't flexible enough in high-dimensional spaces.

Practical Recommendations

  1. A multi-algorithm strategy is mandatory, not optional. ARI can swing by up to 0.95 points between methods on the same dataset (Wine: from −0.005 to 0.946) — a direct consequence of the No Free Lunch Theorem.
  2. Don't use the Silhouette Score as a standalone model-selection metric. Use all three intrinsic metrics together (Silhouette, Davies–Bouldin, Calinski–Harabász) and only trust a conclusion when all three agree; when reference labels are available, prioritize ARI.
  3. Adapt algorithm choice to data dimensionality. Low dimensionality: K-Means/GMM are the first choice. Medium dimensionality: reduce with PCA to 95% explained variance before trying K-Means/GMM Tied. High dimensionality: prefer Agglomerative Ward or GMM Tied after PCA; avoid Complete/Average and DBSCAN.
  4. Read K*Sil ≠ Ktrue as an analytical signal, not a methodological error — investigate overlapping structure via PCA/UMAP rather than mechanically forcing K=Ktrue.
  5. Deploying DBSCAN requires strict noise-ratio control — if it exceeds 20%, DBSCAN is not a good fit for that dataset.
  6. Prefer GMM Tied as the default choice among probabilistic models, thanks to the tied covariance constraint that prevents overfitting on small-to-medium datasets.

Limitations & Future Work

The experimental scope is limited to small-to-medium tabular datasets (150–1,797 samples); the hyperparameter search space (particularly for DBSCAN and Spectral RBF) was not fully explored; and the study only considers clustering on raw features (after scaling/PCA) without exploring learned representations. Four directions for future research are proposed: (1) extending to modern deep clustering methods (DEC, IDEC); (2) evaluating on larger-scale benchmarks (STL-10, CIFAR-10, MIMIC-III); (3) exploring semi-supervised clustering with must-link/cannot-link constraints; (4) building a meta-learning framework to automate algorithm selection based on the input data's meta-features.
