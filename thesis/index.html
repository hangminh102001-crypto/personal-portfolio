MASTER'S THESIS — DATA SCIENCE

Applying Machine Learning Techniques to Detect Fraud in Accounting & Financial Data

Building, deploying, and evaluating an accounting anomaly detection framework that applies modern machine learning techniques to real General Ledger data from an FDI (foreign direct investment) enterprise in Vietnam, while fully complying with current Vietnamese accounting and auditing standards.

Unsupervised Anomaly Detection Deep Autoencoder (PyTorch) Isolation Forest / LOF / KMeans SHAP Explainability Audit & Compliance
The data used in this research (9,804 general-ledger lines, January–December 2022, from an FDI enterprise in the trading sector in Vietnam) was anonymized before being handed over for academic purposes. The company's name, tax code, and specific voucher IDs are not disclosed on this page; the figures shown here are aggregate statistics only.

Why This Research Matters

As Vietnam's economy integrates more deeply into global value chains, the number of foreign direct investment (FDI) enterprises and companies adopting Enterprise Resource Planning (ERP) systems such as SAP, Oracle, and Microsoft Dynamics keeps growing rapidly. According to Vietnam's General Statistics Office (2023), newly registered FDI capital exceeded USD 36.6 billion, while the number of enterprises using ERP for financial management grew by roughly 18% per year. As a result, the volume of accounting data — particularly journal entries recorded in the General Ledger — is increasing at an unprecedented rate. SAP (2023) estimates that around 76% of global transaction revenue flows through one of its ERP systems.

Alongside this growth, accounting fraud is becoming increasingly sophisticated and harder to detect. The Association of Certified Fraud Examiners (ACFE, 2022) estimates that a typical organization loses 5% of annual revenue to fraud, with total global losses exceeding USD 4.7 trillion. PwC (2022) reports that 47% of surveyed organizations experienced at least one fraud incident within a 24-month period. Traditional journal-entry testing methods — Benford's Law testing, "red-flag" tests based on known fraud scenarios, and statistical sampling under ISA 530/VSA 530 — only catch fraud types that are already known, quickly become outdated as fraudsters adapt, and cannot scale efficiently to millions of entries per audit cycle.

The combination of ever-growing ERP data volumes, increasingly sophisticated fraud schemes, and the limitations of traditional methods creates a clear research gap: the need for an unsupervised machine learning framework capable of detecting anomalies across an entire GL dataset without requiring pre-labeled fraud examples, while also integrating the specific legal requirements of the Vietnamese accounting system under Circular 200/2014/TT-BTC, VSA 240, and VSA 530.

Research Object & Scope

The research object is accounting entries recorded in the General Ledger of an FDI enterprise operating under Vietnamese accounting law. The scope covers the full 2022 fiscal year (January–December), comprising 9,804 individual debit/credit ledger lines that were anonymized in strict compliance with data protection requirements. The data reflects the characteristics of an import-export trading company: multi-currency transactions, multiple vendors, and entry structures involving FX revaluation, vendor payments, and inventory postings.

An initial subset covering July–November 2022 (4,218 paired journal entries) — the only part of the dataset with both a voucher date and a separate posting date recorded — was used to validate the anomaly-detection pipeline described below. Extending the pipeline to the full 12-month dataset is in progress.

Experimental Pipeline (per the fraud_detection_v3_1 notebook)

The results below were computed on the Jul–Nov 2022 validation subset (4,218 paired entries) — the currently completed run. The latest pipeline version trains four anomaly-detection models in parallel and combines their outputs through a weighted consensus, rather than processing entries sequentially through separate layers. The underlying theory still follows Schreyer et al. (2017, arXiv:1709.05254) for the AP/RE/AS scoring formulas.

1. Load & clean dataNormalize columns; pair Debit/Credit lines on the same row 4,218 rows, 71 Debit accounts, 48 Credit accounts, 158 Dr–Cr pairs, 106 counterparties
2. Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA)Distribution of log(|amount|); Benford's Law test on the leading digit Benford: statistically significant deviation (p<0.05)
3. Feature Engineering16 features: log-amount, z-scores by account/account-pair, frequency encoding, posting lag, weekend/month-end flags, FX flag... 4,218 × 16 feature matrix, scaled with RobustScaler
4. Train 4 modelsIsolation Forest, LOF, KMeans, Autoencoder (PyTorch: BCEWithLogitsLoss, Xavier init, LeakyReLU 0.4, Dropout 0.1, 3-neuron bottleneck) AE: 30 epochs, final loss 0.2729; each model produces anomaly scores on its own scale

6. Quantitative evaluationSynthetic anomaly injection (simulated GLOBAL/LOCAL anomalies) to compute ROC-AUC across multiple random seeds — needed since the real data has no confirmed fraud labels Designed, not yet run to completion (see note below)

8. SHAP explainabilitySHAP explains why the Autoencoder assigns a high RE score to a given entry Expected top features: Dr-Cr pair frequency, within-pair amount deviation

Illustrative Results

Conclusion

The thesis achieved its stated objectives: (1) successfully building an accounting anomaly detection framework combining Isolation Forest, LOF, KMeans, Autoencoder, and SHAP on real GL data; (2) fully integrating Vietnamese legal requirements (Circular 200/2014 Article 10, VSA 240/530, ISA 530) into the classification logic and output thresholds; (3) successfully classifying the 4,218-entry validation subset into anomaly groups: NORMAL 2,307 (54.7%), GLOBAL 1,024 (24.3%), LOCAL 887 (21.0%); (4) proposing a 5-step CAATs process that can be adopted immediately under Decree 05/2019/ND-CP.

Proposed 5-Step CAATs Process

  1. Data preparation — extract GL data from the ERP system.
  2. Run the ML pipeline — execute the notebook, output classification results with AS scores and SHAP explanations.
  3. Generate a Vietnamese-language Flag Card for each flagged entry: reason for the flag, top SHAP features, and an investigation recommendation.
  4. Internal auditor review — review the Flag Card, confirm or dismiss the flag, and investigate confirmed-risk entries in detail.
  5. Reporting & follow-up — produce an audit report integrating the ML results and update rule-based thresholds for the next cycle.

Limitations

  • Quantitative evaluation (AUC) relies on synthetic anomaly injection, not real fraud labels.
  • Anomaly ≠ fraud — the pipeline's output is a flag for the auditor to review, not a final conclusion.
  • The AP score uses only 4 categorical attributes — some other anomaly types (e.g. time-based) may be missed.
  • Posting lag: year-end entries often show high lag even when legitimate (post-audit adjustments) — this case needs to be excluded from the red-flag rule.
  • The weighted-consensus step is not part of the original Schreyer framework — it follows Bakumenko & Elragal (2022).
  • The thesis is still a work in progress and is currently being finalized.
